How Maya Trains Frontier Voice Models at a Fraction of the Cost

Most of our cost advantage is decided before a single training step — in how speech data is sourced and owned. This document walks the full stack section by section: Data → Labelling & Pipelines → Model Architecture → Self-Improving Loops → RL → How Maya Differs.

01

Data

A voice model is only as good as the speech it learns from. We do not treat data as a commodity to be purchased by the hour — we own its origin. That single decision is the root of both our quality lead and our cost structure.

Why "at source" beats "by the hour"

Licensing pre-packaged corpora from data vendors is a standard, perfectly valid route — most large labs take it. Our difference is not that vendors are wrong; it is that buying finished data gives away the two things that matter most for non-English voice: control over content and control over acoustics. When we collect at source, we decide exactly what is said (knowledge density, domain coverage, conversational style) and how it sounds (dialect, accent, prosody, recording condition) in the same pass. We are not paying a premium to recover information a vendor already stripped out.

This is the heart of our India-first edge. A first-time internet user in Bihar, a shopkeeper in Kolkata, a grandmother in Kochi telling a story — these registers are not in any catalogue. We capture them because we send the recorder to where they are spoken.

Cost structure: no third-party data costs

Because every stream is collected or processed on infrastructure we own, our only data cost is compute. There are no recurring per-minute transcription bills, no licensing fees that scale with corpus size, no vendor lock-in. This matters enormously at our scale: at 3–4M hours, per-minute costs do not add up — they explode.

Illustrative cost model

If we outsourced labelling: 3.5M hours = 210M minutes. At standard commercial ASR / data-vendor rates, even a single transcription pass runs into the millions of dollars — and our council uses multiple passes (Section 2), multiplying that further.

What we actually pay: GPU compute only — the marginal cost of running our own offline ASR / VAD / aligner models. In-housing the stack cuts data-preparation spend by an estimated 85–95% versus the vendor path, and removes a cost that would otherwise grow every time the corpus does.

02

Labelling & In-House Pipelines

Raw audio is not training data. Between collection and the first training step sits a pipeline that cleans, segments, transcribes, aligns, and tags every hour we touch — and we own all of it. This is where 3–4M hours of messy, real-world audio becomes a clean, labelled, timestamped corpus. None of it is outsourced.

The pipeline, end to end

Every hour — internet, human, or app — runs the same owned chain. Each stage is offline and quality-first: unlike a production system, it has no latency budget to respect, so we can run heavier, slower, more accurate models than anyone would ever deploy in real time.

  • VAD & segmentation — detect speech vs silence and noise, then slice long-form audio (podcasts, streams) into clean utterances. Bad segmentation poisons everything downstream, so it runs first — and our in-house VAD is SOTA on our internal Indic test sets.
  • Cleaning & denoise — noise cancellation and source separation produce clean, krisp audio. We deliberately retain controlled-noise variants too — traffic, market, crowd — so the model stays robust to the conditions our users actually speak in.
  • ASR council — several ASR architectures transcribe the same audio in parallel (detailed below).
  • Forced alignment — align every transcript to its audio at word and phoneme level. Precise timestamps are non-negotiable for codec and TTS training, and let us discard anything that doesn't line up.
  • Quality gate — confidence, alignment score, and signal-to-noise thresholds decide what enters training, what gets re-processed, and what's dropped.
  • Native annotation — ⟨N⟩ native annotators tag dialect, emotion, and code-mixing — the judgments models can't make reliably — at source, by people who speak the language.
Why a council of ASRs

No single ASR is good at everything. The conditions that break one model are exactly where another is strong — clean studio vs code-mixed Hindi-English, fast conversational speech vs slow elderly speech, quiet rooms vs traffic and market noise, high-resource vs low-resource languages. The failure modes are different, and that is the entire point.

So we run a council of diverse ASR architectures over the same audio in parallel. Each emits a transcript with a confidence trace, and a consensus layer votes token by token: where the architectures agree, we keep a high-confidence label; where they disagree, that span is routed to a heavier model, to a human, or down-weighted in training. Because the architectures fail in uncorrelated places, the council recovers labels no single model could reach. And because every model is in-house and offline, we can add as many voters as accuracy demands, paying only in compute — the council never has to be fast, it only has to be right.

Quality-first, because it's offline

The labelling stack and the serving stack are built to be opposites. We pour accuracy into labelling once, offline, so the model we actually serve can stay small and cheap forever.

Because every model in the chain is ours, throughput is bounded by GPU fleet, not vendor pricing. Doubling the corpus doubles compute; it does not multiply a per-minute bill. That is the structural reason our data-prep cost stays tied to compute while quality keeps climbing.

Every hour that flows through it makes the next hour cheaper and cleaner — and it all compounds at compute cost only.

Model Architecture

We treat speech as next-token prediction. Audio becomes a short sequence of discrete neural-codec tokens, and a decoder-only transformer predicts them exactly as a language model predicts words. That single decision lets us inherit the entire LLM stack — and then push it somewhere a text model never has to go: throughput-first, inference-heavy, built to be served cheaply at scale.

Speech as next-token prediction

A raw waveform is millions of samples a minute — hopeless to model token by token. So we don't. Our in-house neural codec compresses speech into a handful of codec tokens per frame; the model predicts those tokens, and the codec decodes them back to 24 kHz audio. Sequences stay short, generation streams in real time, and — the part that matters most — every tool built for text LLMs now applies unchanged. We didn't rebuild serving, caching, quantization, or speculative decoding for audio. We inherited them.

The backbone speaks Indian first

We don't start from a blank model. We begin from a strong decoder-only LLM and finetune it hard on modernized Indic text — blogs, web text, and conversation across 10–11 languages and their code-mixing. By the time it ever emits a codec token, the backbone already thinks in these languages: it carries knowledge, idiom, and natural phrasing, not just acoustic mimicry. That text grounding is the difference between a voice that sounds like a local and one that sounds like it's reading a script. The same large Indic LLM later writes our conversational training data — see §4.

From autoregressive to a hybrid Diffusion-LLM

Our first model was a clean autoregressive (AR) decoder: one codec token per forward pass. Excellent quality — but cost scales with length, because every token is another step, and long conversational turns make latency compound. Our latest architecture keeps AR's quality where it earns its place and breaks its sequential bottleneck everywhere else. It is a hybrid Diffusion-LLM, with three decoding modes living in one checkpoint:

  • Autoregressive anchors onset and prosody, where left-to-right causality genuinely carries information.
  • Diffusion (parallel) decoding emits a whole block of codec tokens in parallel — tens at a time — and refines them over a few denoising steps. The number of steps is set by the refinement passes, not by how many tokens the block holds.
  • Self-speculative decoding lets the same weights draft several tokens ahead and verify them in a single pass — no separate draft model to train or host.

For long-form generation we go further, with a novel length-triggered hybrid routing. Below a context-length threshold, dense attention handles local acoustic detail. The moment a conversation runs long and context crosses that threshold, a router activates sparse SSM-based MoE experts — state-space experts that carry the long horizon at linear cost while holding prosody steady across the whole turn. Attention for the near field; SSM experts switched in only when length demands them.

The payoff: quality doesn't drift as conversations stretch out, and cost doesn't explode. Attention scales quadratically with length; the SSM path scales linearly. This is the Nemotron-class direction — AR, diffusion, and self-speculation from a single checkpoint — extended with length-aware routing and pointed squarely at inference-heavy systems.

Built to be served, not just trained

Most labs train for quality and discover serving cost later. We invert it. Our models are quantization-aware trained (QAT) from the start — the network learns to be accurate in INT8/INT4, so the served model is small and fast with almost no quality loss, instead of being squeezed down as a lossy afterthought. Serving then runs throughput-first: vLLM, continuous batching, KV and prefix caching, parallel decode — all tuned to maximize concurrent real-time streams per GPU rather than single-request vanity latency.

This is the through-line of the entire document: training is where we spend; inference is where we save. We train small models hard on millions of hours, then quantize, parallelize, and speculate so a single GPU carries many live conversations at once.

Why this architecture wins
  • Throughput-first — block-parallel decoding plus self-speculation deliver far more tokens/sec at fixed latency, so each GPU serves more streams and every conversation costs less.
  • Quality kept — AR anchoring with diffusion refinement preserves prosody and naturalness; we don't trade voice quality for speed.
  • Cheap long-form — the SSM–attention hybrid keeps long conversations linear in cost, not quadratic.
  • One checkpoint — AR, diffusion, and self-speculation from a single set of weights: no separate draft model, lower memory, simpler ops.
  • Small & quantized — QAT models run on a single small or edge GPU, which is what makes India-scale serving affordable.

Self-Improving Loops & Synthetic Advantage

Everyone has the same scaling laws. We run them backwards. Most labs scale up to a big model and then serve it. We deliberately train large models we have no intention of serving — they are factories, not products — and use them to manufacture the data that makes a small, cheap model punch far above its size.

Scaling laws, run backwards

The standard recipe pushes parameters and data toward a frontier model you then deploy. Ours inverts the last step. The large model's only job is to generate and teach: it writes conversations, synthesizes speech, and distills its competence into the small model we actually ship. Scale is something we spend upstream, once, so that inference stays small forever.

The writer: an in-house finetuned Indic LLM

At the top sits an open-source LLM that we finetuned in-house on Indic text scraped from modern blogs and web writing — tuned to sound natural and contemporary, not stilted or textbook. Across 10–11 languages and their code-mixing, it follows instructions and writes knowledge-dense conversational text: friendly chats, daily check-ins, question-and-answer, domain talk. This is an intelligible, instruction-following writer that is entirely ours — no external script vendor sits in the loop.

The factory: text becomes synthetic speech

The writer generates text; our large in-house expressive TTS — prosodic, conversational, with non-repetitive, seed-controllable voices — renders it into audio. Because voices are seed-controllable and never repeat, we manufacture effectively unlimited, diverse synthetic conversation at compute cost. We then combine that synthetic data with real data and train a smaller model on more total data than it could ever collect alone. The result: small models that get perfected to almost the same performance as models 2–3× their size.

Distillation for inference, and for speculation

We distill big → small in-house: the small served model learns from the teachers' outputs (synthetic data) alongside real and pseudo-labelled data. This is purely an inference-efficiency move — and it pays a second dividend. A small model that closely tracks a larger teacher is exactly what self-speculative decoding (§3) needs: cheap, accurate drafts that get verified in a single pass. The loop that makes the model small is the same loop that makes it fast.

Why other labs spend more here

Conventional pipelines buy data, license scripts, or pay per-call for a third-party model to generate or judge — costs that recur with every batch. Our writer, our TTS, and our distillation are all in-house, so synthetic data is bounded only by compute, not by invoices.

And it compounds: real data anchors truth, synthetic data scales coverage, one big model amortizes into many small ones, and the served model's real conversations feed the next round. Cost stays tied to compute; capability keeps climbing.

Reinforcement Learning

The data loop gets a small model most of the way. RL takes it the rest. We run RL heavy — across many controlled environments, with humans in the loop, over all 11 languages, for days at a time — to lift a small model to the capability of one 2–3× its size.

Teacher distillation, then reinforcement

RL continues the big → small story. In-house teacher and reward models score the student's conversations and provide the targets it learns from — there is no third-party judge or reward API anywhere in the loop. The teacher's policy is distilled into the small model, then sharpened by reinforcement against signals we own.

Human-in-the-loop, all 11 languages, for days

Scoring and RLHF run with humans in the loop — native speakers judging naturalness, correctness, and feel — across multiple controlled environments and all 11 languages, for multiple days per cycle. This is not a token pass at the end of training; it is a heavy, scaled stage, and native judgment at every step is exactly the part an English-first lab can't cheaply staff.

What RL sharpens — and what's next

Today, RL targets conversational quality and long-horizon stability — holding a real conversation across many turns without drifting — plus the naturalness layer that makes speech feel alive: backchanneling, natural pauses, and turn-taking. On the near roadmap: tool calling and personas. The architecture and the loop are already built to absorb both.

The payoff: punching 2–3× above its weight

The combined effect — the synthetic data loop of §4 plus heavy, human-guided RL — lifts the small served model to the capability of a model 2–3× its size trained without RL. We get frontier-class conversation at a fraction of the serving cost, which is the entire point of building this way.
